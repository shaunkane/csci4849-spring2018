    Distinctions between groups

    • Born blind vs. became blind
    • "Total blindness" vs., for example, light perception
    • In my experience, these distinctions usually don't matter as much as we would think
    • Do all sighted people care what color their shirt is?
    • Are all sighted people good at reading maps?
    • Other differentiating factors: What AT do they use? What is the task and what are the functional challenges?
    • But, some surprises: For example, did the person learn to write?

      Slide 15

      Big ideas: low vision

      • People with low vision use a very different set of technologies than others
      • Where we can, take advantage of user's visual abilities
      • Solutions will often involve customizable user interfaces
      Slide 16

      What is low vision anyway?

      • An estimated 253 million people live with vision impairment: 36 million (14%) are blind and 217 million (85%) have moderate to severe vision impairment
      • 81% of people who are blind or have moderate or severe vision impairment are aged 50 years and above
      • Data from World Health Organization

      Links

      • World Health Organization stats
      Slide 17

      Legally blind?

      • Defined in USA as:
      • Visual acuity of 20/200 or less (in "best" eye) with correction
      • Or a visual field of 20 degrees or less (vs. ~190 degrees for typical vision)

      Sources: - http://www.afb.org/info/blindness-statistics/key-definitions-of-statistical-terms/25 - https://stanford.edu/class/ee267/lectures/lecture5.pdf

      20/200? This means that what the person sees at 20 feet is as clear as what a person with "normal" vision sees at 200 feet. For more details, see https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Visual_acuity

        Slide 21

        Common challenges

        • Seeing fine details
        • Differentiating colors or patterns
        • Visual comparisons (when zoomed in)
        • Peripheral vision and group awareness
        • Increased sensitivity to environmental effects: glare, brightness, darkness

        As always, we should think about activities and how they might be impeded. For example, working in the kitchen can involve distance vision, or comparing multiple items side by side.

        Slide 22

        Using technology

        • Often prefer vision over screen reader (why?)
        • Typically use magnification
        • É and sometimes high-contrast mode
        • Even when screen readers could be more efficient
        • May involve use of a magnifying lens, or placing face close to the screen
        • Need support both for computer screens and other documents (paper documents, projection screens, etc.)

        Why prefer vision? Audio is tough to learn (as you know); it breaks down for complex spatial tasks; it can be stigmatizing; hard to use in public; may lack training.

        The point here is that we can't assume that a person will use the most efficient method.

        Slide 27

        Other ways low vision users are different

        • Many potential manifestations: blurry vision, limited field of view, etc.
        • Can be progressive (e.g. Usher Syndrome)
        • Comorbid conditions: deafness (deafblind), cognitive, motor, aging
        • May not have blindness training skills (Braille, white cane, screen readers)

        • Limited field of view
        • Occluded areas
        • Blurriness
        • Lack of contrast
        • Limited near or far vision

          Limitations of simulation

          • 'New research findings from the University of Colorado indicate that blindness simulations Ð intended to be bridge-builders resulting in greater compassion and understanding Ð can sometimes harm rather than help. According to the authors, simulation activities, and blindness simulations in particular, "highlight the initial challenges of becoming disabled" and thus "decrease the perceived adaptability of being disabled and reduce the judged capabilities of disabled people."'

          Slide 33

          How to handle low vision on the web

          • Offer font size buttons
          • Offer a high-contrast or large print stylesheet
          • Users may adopt their own custom stylesheet
          Slide 34

          Research challenges

          • Low vision AT often underexplored in research (why?)
          • Partial vision can even be a deterrent to participating in research
          • Big challenges: Better magnification, better UI support for magnification, help with finding stuff

          Why might people with low vision not be included in research? Easier to focus on lowest common denominator. Research studies may want people with consistent visual ability, but there is a lot of variation. Low vision tech often requires significant UI customization, which we are not good at.

            Slide 38

            Visual design for older adults

            • Design with high visual contrast
            • Allow users to change text size without breaking anything
            • AARP recommends providing on-screen controls for changing text size (why?)

            Link: AARP Design Guidelines in PDF format

            Why provide text size buttons? A few reasons: lets the designer decide how to implement changes. But most importantly, it is more discoverable.

            Slide 39

            Color blindness

            • Affects about 10% of men; fewer women
            • Difficulty distinguishing colors is the primary functional challenge
            • Several "types" of color blindness, but individual differences remain

            Color blind people don't actually see the world in black and white. Instead, they tend to see colors that most people recognize as different colors, as a single color.

            Slide 40

              The important point is not that color blind people have weird vision. The point is that this can affect what people are trying to do, like identifying a specific bird, or picking a ripe tomato.

              Images from Bored Panda. Left: 4 copies of a photo showing three parrots; showing normal vision, deuteranomaly, protanopia, and tritanopia. Right: 4 copies of an image of tomatoes with the same 4 conditions.

              Slide 41

              Designing for color blindness

              • Don't use color alone to distinguish items
              • Instead, combine with shape, texture, or some other factor
              • Avoid a special color blind friendly mode; make it work out of the box

              It is possible to test for the different types of color blindness and accommodate them, but some users might still be unable to use your product due to their unique color vision. It is better just to have a backup way of differentiating items.

              Image: Trello offers color blind friendly mode which changes the visual texture of labels. The only thing they could do better would be to make this by default.

                                      Deafblindness

                                        Slide 70

                                        Deafblindness

                                        • Combine some vision loss with some hearing loss
                                        • Between 70,000 and 100,00 in the US
                                        • About 50% have a condition called Usher Syndrome
                                        • Born deaf, lose vision over time (especially peripheral vision)
                                        • May grow up speaking sign languages like ASL; may learn tactile sign language and Braille
                                        • Typically use Braille for computer access

                                        Link: Color Blindness FAQ from the American Association of the Deaf-Blind
