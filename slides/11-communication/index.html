      AAC

      • Augmentative and Alternative Communication
      • Covers a very wide range of techniques and abilities
      • More than most assistive technology, there is no one size fits all for AAC
      Slide 7

      Why AAC is interesting to HCI

      • Many different types of interaction challenges (physical, cognitive, linguistic)
      • Natural human communication is fast, complex, multi-modal
      • Communication skills reflect upon self image, interpersonal relations
      • Communication is very contextual—may be able to leverage sensing, linguistics

        Slide 10

        Who uses AAC

        • Cognitive impairment (congenital or acquired, as in traumatic brain injury)
        • Autism spectrum
        • Aphasia
        • Physically unable to speak (ALS, other severe physical impairments)
        • Unable to speak clearly: dysarthric speech
        • Very often involves comorbid disabilities

        These are really different!

        Slide 11

        Many interaction challenges

        • Articulating spoken words
        • Remembering the appropriate words (for written and/or spoken text)
        • Recognizing others' words
        • Using appropriate grammar
        • Selecting the words you want to say
        • By typing it out
        • By recognizing it on a screen
        • All of this is compounded by difficulties in using a graphical user interface

        Not everyone will encounter all of these challenges. But these can suggest really different solutions.

                Slide 26

                Amyotrophic Lateral Sclerosis (ALS)

                • Affects 1 in 50,000 adults
                • Progressive neurodegenerative disease with 2-5 year life expectancy
                • Speech and motor movement often isignificantly mpaired, eye gaze primary input method
                • Reliant on AAC for most communication

                Affects 1 in 50,000 in U.S. Adult onset Progressive neurodegenerative disease, no cure, 2 – 5 year life expectancy Eye movement typically remains, so eye gaze is common form of input

                Slide 27

                Using AAC with eye gaze

                • Typical communication rate 10-20 words per minute (wpm)
                • Spoken speech approximately 180 wpm
                • Error rate can be high, especially in specific locations , including outdoors
                • Users typically have low-tech methods as a backup (hand signals or e-tran boards, yes/no gestures)
                Slide 28

                  Despite the challenges, for people with ALS, improving assistive technology can have a real positive impact on their lives. Looking to technology to help.

                      Slide 33

                      Issues with real-time communication

                      • Pacing
                      • Volume
                      • Pronunciation
                      • Conversational roles
                      Slide 34

                      Conversational pacing

                      • Slow rate of output commonly affected PALS' ability to keep up with communication; especially with strangers (20 vs. 180 wpm)
                      • A2's spouse: "others are a little unnerved, break out the phone [mimics texting] because... of downtime while he's formulating a response..."
                      • Lack of awareness of when the AAC user is typing or idle
                      • A2 needs to "burst in" to conversations
                      • A6 interjects "but" or "also" to indicate a longer sentence is coming
                      Slide 35

                      Phrase-level pacing

                      • AAC typically provided little control over timing within a sentence or paragraph
                      • A1: "when I hit speak, there is no pause between my sentences [where I put a hyphen], and that would piss me off because it becomes a run-on... that forces me to type a sentence, hit speak, delete it, and retype a new one…"
                      • A3: "no sense of humor... it [humor] just doesn't come across in the same way... it [the speech rendering] doesn't pause where you would pause, that's a really big one."

                      Affects humor, storytelling Forced to use workarounds

                      Slide 36

                      Volume and pronunciation

                      • Lack of control over volume limited PALS' ability to communicate in public or in groups
                      • A1: "I can't go to a restaurant or somewhere with traffic because I can't project my voice [over the ambient noise]. I have a speaker, an amplifier, but it's cumbersome and I never have it when I really need it..."
                      • AAC often mispronounces words, especially foreign language words, acronyms ("ALS"), and names. PALS learned how to misspell words to achieve correct pronunciation.

                      A2 had to misspell his wife's name Misspelling can affect word prediction! ALS "alls" vs A.L.S.

                      Slide 37

                      Change in conversational roles

                      • PALS often struggled with a shift to a more passive role
                      • A2's wife: "I think his family as a whole... they're animated people, so I would say the whole sibling engagement was very spontaneous and there was a lot of banter... and I can definitely say that some have not made the adjustment to silence-engagement-silence-engagement ... I think it's uncomfortable [for them]."
                      • Maintaining an active role often required additional work
                      • A3's partner: "if somebody's coming over, he'll write an agenda of questions for them... because otherwise it's really awkward...especially for somebody who doesn't come over very often and is not very comfortable with the pauses."

                      A3 prepared top ten lists and interview Qs

                      Slide 38

                      Issues with expressing personality

                      • Controlling tone and expressing emotion
                      • Humor
                      • Storytelling
                      • Communicating concisely
                      Slide 39

                      Tone and emotion

                      • PALS expressed frustration about their monotone AAC voice, often perceived as emotionless or presenting negative emotions ("condescending" and "less compassionate")
                      • A1: "that mood ... when you read a text from someone you don't know well... that now is how I'm starting to feel with everyone."
                      • PALS felt unable to express their emotions, while feeling emotional could impact the AAC accuracy
                      • A6: "I have to write the words 'I'm disappointed', or 'I'm getting frustrated.'"
                      • A7: "Strong emotion hinders Tobii usage, tears confuse eyegaze technology and anger prevents focus."
                      • Recorded speech was valued, but not all were able to voice bank in time
                      Slide 40

                      Humor

                      • Humor is impaired by the inability to provide quick responses, limited control over pacing, inability to express sarcasm
                      • A7: "Each day presents countless frustrations as humor and incisive comments require timing and inflection."
                      • PALS found new ways to express humor by changing their AAC voice (changing gender, adding a southern drawl), creative cursing, storing and replaying amusing quotes and songs

                      A6 said sarcasm was his second priority

                      Slide 41

                      Storytelling

                      • Some PALS had previously been known as great storytellers, and had difficulty telling stories with their AAC
                      • Some wrote extensive stories, but found that their AAC did not provide control over pacing or voices
                      • A5: "all my voices sound the same."
                      • PALS stored favorite quotes, and played videos to spark conversations
                      • A6's caregiver: "[he] shows off a lot of pictures and videos... that's an easy way for him to show something without having to describe it or talk about it. He's just like, 'look at this.'"

                      A5 was known to imitate others

                      Slide 42

                      Communicating concisely

                      • PALS found that the constraints of their AAC required them to speak more deliberately and concisely
                      • A2's wife: "the bluntness is kind of cool, too, because he doesn't have time to beat around the bush," and, "It might be rude if you and I say it, but for him to say it, it's much more ... socially acceptable to say."
                      • A5: "Somewhere along the line I think I just stopped thinking about it too much, stopped caring about it too much, just worried about getting the point across."
                      • A7: "I think one arena that's improved as a result of AAC use, is my ability to express myself succinctly, when I must."

                      Slide 44

                      Communicating in public

                      • PALS often have worked out communication strategies with family, but had more difficulty with strangers, who were not used to communicating with AAC users
                      • PALS experienced difficulty expressing private comments in public, as they can't "whisper"; use IM or facial gestures

                      Some non-native speakers also

                      Slide 45

                      Pets and agents

                      • Pets: One participant (A4) "can no longer call the cat" because the cat did not recognize his AAC output as his speech
                      • Agents: Voice agents like Siri and Alexa typically could not understand PALS' dysarthric speech, but they could use their AAC to speak to these devices

                      A5: AAC -> Siri -> message

                      Slide 46

                      Interacting with children

                      • Communicating with children was, as children often could not interpret emotion from context:
                      • A6: "It's almost impossible to express frustration when I'm trying to get [my son] to hustle up and get ready... So I have to use words like '[son], you need to hurry.'"
                      • A5 reads bedtime stories to his children using AAC; A2 used to sing to his children but now plays nostalgic songs

                      Also cannot read, so no private communication

                      Slide 47

                      Online interactions

                      • Writing, journaling, and interacting online took on added significance for many PALS
                      • A2: "[I] never wrote before. Now I write like I used to talk."
                      • A6 writes journal entries for his son; A7 blogs and reviews books
                      • PALS withdrew from some online communities because they involved activities the PALS could no longer participate in
                      • A7: "Prior to ALS taking away the use of my limbs, I spent hours on photography and photography-related applications… Now, I'm unable to access any of it."

                        A hierarchy of expressive AAC

                          Present oneself

                          Slide 51

                          Beyond WPM: New Metrics for AAC

                          • Authenticity: Does AAC communication effectively express the user's personality, communication style, and sense of humor (as it existed before ALS)?
                          • Situational Correctness: Can the user switch between formal/informal communication to match the context? (e.g., punctuation norms, bilingualism, slang, cursing, wordplay)
                          • Expressing and Accommodating Emotion: Can the user control tone and pacing, and dynamically change emotions during a conversation? Can they use the AAC when feeling extreme emotions?
                          • Goal-Oriented Speech: Can the AAC support directed social activities (e.g., negotiating to buy the family car, running a business meeting)?
                          • Conversational Metrics: Does the AAC support equal participation, turn taking, communication with non-native speakers, children, pets/?

                          AAC turing test – can I differentiate communication before/after ALS

                          Slide 52

                          Enhancing AAC expressivity

                          • Awareness displays representing the current state of the AAC device (see Kiley Sobel et al., CHI 17)
                          • Support greater control over performance of sentences and stories
                          • Support smoother integration between pre-recorded and live content
                          Slide 53

                          Conclusion

                          • Lots of room for improving the performance of AAC
                          • Challenges related to physical, environmental, and social factors
                          • In parallel, we should consider how AAC can improve the user's ability to express themselves authentically
                          • Despite challenges, participants are often able to adapt their use of AAC to express their personality
